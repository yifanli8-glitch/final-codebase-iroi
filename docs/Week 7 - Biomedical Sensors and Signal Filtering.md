One Wire Temperature Sensor - DS18B20
The DS18B20 is a digital temperature sensor that provides highly accurate temperature 
readings through the One-Wire communication protocol, requiring only a single data line 
for connection.
It offers a measurement range of −55 °C to +125 °C with up to 0.0625 °C resolution, making 
it ideal for IoT, environmental monitoring, and embedded systems.
Inside the sensor, a silicon diode changes predictably with temperature — its forward 
voltage decreases by about 2 mV per °C — and the DS18B20 measures this voltage 
change electronically, then converts it into a precise digital temperature reading through its 
internal circuit.

Last Week - Adjustment Calibration (Error Compensation)
In real practice, sensor readings generally has errors. We can test the 
characteristics of the error and make compensation. This is called adjustment 
calibration. 
For example, if a temperature sensor always reads 5 °C on ice and 105 °C on 
boiling water, it has a bias of + 5 °C. We can simply add - 5 °C to all sensor 
readings to compensate the bias.

Electrocardiogram (ECG or EKG)
An ECG (Electrocardiogram) sensor measures the electrical activity of the 
heart through electrodes placed on the skin, by detecting voltage differences
(several mV before amplification) generated by the heart’s depolarization and 
repolarization cycles and converting them into measurable electrical signals.
ECG is widely used in medical diagnosis, fitness monitoring, and wearable 
health devices to assess heart rate, rhythm, and cardiac health.

Photoplethysmogram (PPG or Pleth)
A PPG (Photoplethysmography) sensor is an optical device used to measure 
blood volume changes in the microvascular tissue, by emitting light into the 
skin and detecting the amount of light reflected or absorbed, which varies with 
the pulsatile flow of blood.
PPG is used in smartwatches, fitness trackers, and medical monitors to 
measure heart rate, blood oxygen (SpO₂), and vascular health.

Pulse Arrival Time (PAT)
Pulse Arrival Time (PAT) is the time delay between the heart’s electrical 
activity (from ECG) and the arrival of the corresponding pulse wave (from 
PPG) at a peripheral site, often used to estimate blood pressure and vascular

Electroencephalogram (EEG)
An EEG (Electroencephalogram) sensor measures the electrical activity of the 
brain through electrodes placed on the scalp. It works by detecting tiny voltage 
fluctuations (10 to 100 µV) produced by synchronized neuronal firing in the 
cerebral cortex.
EEG is widely used in neurology, brain–computer interfaces, sleep studies, 
depth of anesthesia, and mental health research to monitor brain states and 
diagnose neurological conditions.

Electromyography (EMG)
An EMG (Electromyography) sensor measures the electrical activity produced 
by muscle contractions, by detecting voltage changes generated by motor 
unit action potentials (50 µV to 5 mV) through surface or intramuscular 
electrodes.
EMG is commonly used in rehabilitation, prosthetic control, sports science, 
and robotics to analyze muscle function and movement intention.

Noise in Biomedical Sensors
Measurements of bio-information are often small, and are subject to noises. 
Thermal noise – random voltage fluctuations due to resistor and component heat. 
Amplifier noise – amplifier can also amplify noise.
Cross-talk – interference from nearby sensors or muscle groups.
Motion artifacts – body or cable movement inducing mechanical or electrical 
disturbances.

Signal Processing 
Signal processing is the technique of analyzing, modifying, and interpreting 
signals—such as electrical, acoustic, or biological waveforms—to extract 
meaningful information or improve quality. 
It involves operations like filtering, amplification, feature extraction, and 
transformation to remove noise and highlight useful patterns.
In biomedical systems, signal processing enables the clean measurement of 
physiological signals such as ECG, EMG, and PPG, making them suitable for 
diagnosis, monitoring, and control applications.

Low-Pass Filter
A low-pass filter is an electronic or digital tool that lets slow (low-frequency) 
changes pass through while blocking fast (high-frequency) fluctuations or 
noise.
It is commonly used to smooth signals with small fluctuations like heart rate, 
temperature, or sound by removing unwanted rapid variations.

Low-Pass Filter – Exponential Smoothing
Exponential smoothing is a filtering method that reduces noise by giving more 
weight to recent data points while exponentially decreasing the influence of 
older ones. 
Sk = αxk + (1 - α) Sk-1
[ new filtered reading = α X new raw reading + (1- α) X last filtered reading ]
where S is the filtered signal, x is the original signal and α is the exponential factor.

Anonymous Data or De-identified?
Some ECG waveforms are recorded from a hospital. The record also contains the 
time and operating room number, but no information of patient is recorded. Is this 
recording anonymous or de-identified? Why? 
The data will be anonymous if no one can use the time and room number to trace 
back to the patient. But in real practice, the recording is more likely to be de-identified. 
Because most of hospitals have electronic medical records (EMR) systems so that 
doctors can trace back the patient in EMR system, once the time and operating 
number is known.
Anonymous data means that no one has any ways to directly or indirectly trace 
back to any individual or any group of people.
